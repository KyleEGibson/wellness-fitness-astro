Foam rolling has a reputation problem: done wrong, it just feels like self-inflicted pain without much benefit. Done correctly, it's a genuinely useful recovery tool that can reduce soreness and improve mobility between workouts.

## Slow Down — Speed Defeats the Purpose

The most common mistake is rolling back and forth quickly, treating it like a massage chair. Instead, roll slowly, spending 20-30 seconds on each muscle group, pausing longer on any spot that feels particularly tight or tender.

## Breathe Through Tender Spots, Don't Avoid Them

When you hit a genuinely tight or tender area, resist the urge to roll past it quickly. Instead, hold gentle, sustained pressure on that spot for 20-30 seconds while breathing deeply — this is often where foam rolling delivers the most benefit.

## Timing Matters More Than People Think

Foam rolling before a workout can help with mobility and blood flow, but light pressure is key — aggressive pre-workout rolling can temporarily reduce muscle strength output. Save deeper, more thorough rolling for after a workout or on rest days.

## Avoid Rolling Directly on Joints or Lower Back

Foam rollers work on muscle tissue, not joints or the spine. Rolling directly over your knees, lower back vertebrae, or neck can cause discomfort or minor injury — focus on the muscle groups surrounding these areas instead.

## Consistency Beats Intensity

Rolling for 5-10 minutes several times a week produces better long-term mobility and soreness reduction than one intense 30-minute session followed by weeks of nothing. Treat it as a regular habit alongside your workouts, not an occasional deep-tissue intervention.
